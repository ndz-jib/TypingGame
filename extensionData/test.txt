"Scenery" represents the castration of historical facts under the "subjective perspective," a canvas whitewashed through "internalization," often resisting external "pressure" as "the return of the repressed." Yet this blank outlet may also be misled by the "source of pressure"—the "empty place." This museum, to establish itself, must first negate the very concept of "museum," thereby ceding exhibition rights to the subject's "scenery," from which "internalization" emerges. Ultimately, the repressed subject outsources this "internalized" compensation to "the higher," transforming "representation does not equal transfer of power" into equality. Thus, the aesthetics of historical wounds become the origin and catastrophe of the nation—this is precisely the evolution of narrative, a "packaging/outsourcing" technique after selecting the (narrative) "subject." Humans cannot literally seal themselves into "delivery boxes," but once a narrative subject is chosen, the subject of the Real is handed over to language for "packaging." By severing material connections to the Real (historical context, ethical responsibility, etc.), it becomes a foreign object, an existence awaiting "packaging = definition." Yet the transmission of "narrative" is a process of "outsourcing," where the estrangement of the subject creates the illusion of "handing over to the higher," and as long as "narrative" exists, the dream of this so-called "higher" becomes immortal, transforming into "myth." At this point, the ritual required by "myth" is to "pack/outsource" the narrative subject. Fundamentally, "subjectivity" has already been lost, handed over as a symbol to be filled in by "the higher myth"—this is the most dangerous aspect. Whoever can promise an immortal "myth" becomes the priest, a "higher" figure manipulating and playing the role of "divinity" from behind. Even if this promiser, as a symbol, could be replaced by anyone capable of constructing divine discourse, the "illusion" persists forever, bewitching all within the "narrative structure."
